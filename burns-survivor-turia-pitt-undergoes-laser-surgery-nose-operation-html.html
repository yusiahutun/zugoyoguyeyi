Burns survivor Turia Pitt undergoes laser surgery before nose operation

Publish date: 2024-01-30

Burns survivor and mum-of-one Turia Pitt, 31, has undergone laser surgery ahead of a nose operation and revealed she did with this without having a general anaesthetic.

The Australian athlete suffered burns to 60 per cent of her body after she was caught in a bushfire in 2011 during an ultramarathon.

She recently took to Instagram to say the laser procedure was advised by her surgeon before he operated on her nose again.

'I went in on the 'awake' surgery list which means that I was wide awake during the whole process (normally I'd go under general anaesthetic),' Turia captioned the post.

In 2011, Turia was caught in a horrific bushfire and suffered burns to 65 per cent of her body

What happened to Turia?

* Turia Pitt, a former model and mining engineer, was 24 when she was caught in a grass fire that left her with burns to 65 per cent of her body.

* She had been competing in an ultramarathon when fires swept through Kimberley, West Australia.

* As well as suffering severe burns, Turia lost several fingers and spent a gruelling five months in hospital. During this time she was placed in an induced coma. She also received numerous skin grafts.

* She had major reconstructive surgery to her nose in 2015 and again in 2018.

'This time a simple assortment of 7 local anaesthetic needles and a veritable boatload of numbing cream did the trick.

'After all that I couldn't feel how much drool was coming out of my mouth, but we still managed to laser my nose/face area without too much pain.

She said while she found the process a 'tad traumatising', she'd opted for an 'awake' surgery as it was quicker and didn't leave her feeling as 'knocked around'. 

As well as revealing details of her laser procedure, Turia also told her followers what she did to aid her recovery.

The first stage of her healing process saw her spend 10 minutes under her Bioptron lamp at least three times a day.

'They used this on me in hospital to promote wound healing. It has biostimulative effects. I'm not really sure what that means but, in my anecdotal experience, it's helped me heal quicker and faster, which means less downtime.' 

'Once my skin is pretty much healed, I’m back to my normal skin regime (rubbing diamonds on my face, bathing in unicorn tears,' she said displaying her trademark humour

To assist her skin as it recovered, Turia said applied plain liquid paraffin - despite what others told her to do.

'I think that my doctors and surgeons are pretty savvy, and if an entire medical unit recommends liquid paraffin, I listen to them.'

She said she kept her skin hydrated using Avène’s thermal spring water and when as it was healing applied Avène’s Cicalfate Post Procedure emulsion.

'Once my skin is pretty much healed, I’m back to my normal skin regime (rubbing diamonds on my face, bathing in unicorn tears etc),' she said displaying her trademark humour. 

Turia is a mum-of-one to her son Hakavai, a baby she had in December 2017 (pictured Turia and Hakavai)

Turia's brush with death in 2011 saw her suffer burns to 65 per cent of her body, lose several fingers and spend a gruelling five months in hospital.

She had her nose rebuilt in 2015, a surgery that ended dramatically after she went into cardiac arrest.

Three years later, Turia underwent another nose reconstructive surgery, revealing at the time to her social media followers the procedure allowed her to breathe through her nostrils for the first time in five years. 

New mother Turia Pitt (pictured with son Hakavai) has undergone a nose reconstruction

'I won't go into too much detail, because the gore factor is high, but basically the doctors took cartilage from my ear and used it to re-shape my nose. Amazing, huh?! 

'I choose to keep having surgeries like this because I believe life is all about continual improvement. And, whatever you do in life, you can't improve without doing the work.'

Turia has touched countless people's lives through her books where she has shared details of her journey of surviving burns.

She has also documented her recovery openly on social media and through her newsletters.

'For me ''doing the work'' means accepting that I'm going to be in a bit of pain, recognising that I'm not going to be able to do the things I enjoy for a while (running/surfing/being active), taking time off from work and other projects, and trusting the process,' she added.

'But putting in the work always pays off. I can breathe through my nostrils for the first time in five years and I'm loving hanging with the best antidote in the world. 

'I'm jotting down some thoughts about how to keep putting in the work, even if you don't know how it will turn out.'

Dallas' Larry Hagman's daughter tells how he was a serial cheater, a drunk and a pothead

Publish date: 2024-08-05

Actor Larry Hagman, best known for playing the ruthless oil baron JR Ewing in the long running soap opera, Dallas, had a reputation for being a happy go-lucky guy who wanted to play, dance, sing, drink and get high.

But behind his costume wearing, dope-smoking, drinking openly and hippie lifestyle, Hagman led a double life with shocking secrets that even his daughter, Kristina Hagman only learned after his death in November 2012.

Kristina said that growing up, she saw several of her father's close friends and lovers nude while partying at their Malibu, California, home

Hagman always talked about his stable marriage of 58 years with his Swedish-born wife, Maj Axelsson - he didn't want to be like his Dallas TV character guilty of endless philandering and scheming.

In reality, that was also Hagman's personality.

'Dad liked secrets. Secrets made him feel powerful and in control', Kristina Hagman writes in The Eternal Party: Understanding My Dad, Larry Hagman, the TV Star America Loved to Hate, published by Thomas Dunne.

One of his best-kept secrets was that all of his lovers - which included close family friends who came to dinner often - regularly jumped naked into his Jacuzzi together and dominated the living room of their Malibu, California, home.

'Dad had lovers like this - people we all knew, people who were a part of our lives who regularly had dinner with family but were also his special intimate friends'.

When Hagman died, the lovers showed up in force in Dallas and Los Angeles to honor their deceased and adored lover. They confessed to Kristina that her dad was the most important man in their lives.

Hagman, nicknamed the Mad Monk of Malibu, boasted that his favorite secret keeper was Texas oil tycoon HL Hunt, whose secret family and 14 children were uncovered after he died.

While Hagman's daughter had sensed years earlier that there were other women, she never wanted to confront her father and interrupt his 'Don't Worry! Be Happy! Feel Good!' joyride through life.

'He was such a good bulls*****r,' she wrote in her book. 'He was so good at making things up that I never knew what was real and true and what was not.'

His devoted wife, Maj Axelsson, was a fashion designer in London when the couple met. 

She could do anything from cooking great feasts to creating fantasy costumes for Hagman, renovating their home and even building houses. 

She kept the money coming in during those early lean years when Hagman was looking for work.

Axelsson devoted her life to keeping Hagman happy and 'although Larry spoke glowingly of her to strangers and the press, when talking to people who were truly close to him, he blamed her for any relationship in their life together that went wrong', Kristina writes.

'It was Dad who often cut people from our lives suddenly or had quasi-secret relationships with strange people whom no one in the family dared ask questions about,' Kristina writes.

When a trusted family friend was suddenly 'out' of the inner circle, Hagman's answer was 'Maj just didn't not like them'.

But the truth was he was using Maj 'to hide his own anger or sudden distrust of people they had become close to'.

The strange tribe of people always in his company never suspected that Hagman, who appeared to be endlessly happy and put on a flag-waving parade every night at sunset along the water's edge in front of their Malibu house, had a mean streak he blamed on Maj.

Several nights a week there were big dinner parties at Hagman's house that included a lot of drinking followed by a naked romp in the Jacuzzi.

A free flowing open bar was well stocked with inexpensive champagne, wine and beer.

After dessert, everyone danced in front of a nine-by-12-foot stained glass window lit from behind, illuminating giant, psychedelic flowers, mushrooms and butterflies made by Maj's sister Lillimor.

A disco ball covered the partygoers with tiny white sparkles - adding to the psychedelic atmosphere.

'People loved coming to our house' and they all had a long soak in the indoor Jacuzzi, Kristina writes.

'Almost everyone who ever walked through our door was someone I saw wet and naked before they left our house,' she added. 

Once out of the Jacuzzi, they put on hooded terry clothes robes made by Maj and her sisters. 

Hagman called them monks' robes, but said that they were more regal with decorative gold trim, Kristina writes.

'Getting naked into the Jacuzzi with my parents and my brother, Preston… was a slice of our everyday life,' she adds.

A caption next to a photograph of the happy bathing family in the book featured a quote from Hagman: 'The family that bathes together stays together'.

The family would also take psychedelic drugs together.

While friends and lovers viewed the Hagman lifestyle as utopian with wine, women, music and great food, there were some who attempted to abuse that 'freewheeling lifestyle'.

'These were the people who failed to recognize that being naked in our Jacuzzi did not give them a license to have sex with everyone they laid their eyes on,' writes Kristina.

'People roamed around the house at all times of the day and night, and as I approached puberty, I learned to tell drunk and stoned men of every kind to get out of my room,' she adds.

One afternoon, bad boy actor Dennis Hopper showed up uninvited with two women and John Paul Getty III, grandson of the oil tycoon, when Hagman happened to be out of town. 

Hagman was on the outs with Hopper at the time for stiffing his close friend, Peter Fonda out of royalties due from the hit movie, Easy Rider.

Getty got stoned and started played loud music. He told Kristina they were high on acid and need to hang out until they came down.

Suddenly Dennis noticed Kristina and started following her around the house. When she refused to sit down and talk with him, he told the women with him, 'Get her in the Jacuzzi. I want to f*** her'.

She knew he was serious. She grabbed the car keys, ran upstairs to her room, climbed out a window onto the roof, shimmied down a tree. She made her escape in the family car and drove to a friend's house.

But this wasn't Kristina's first sexual threat in her own home. Prior to living in Malibu, the Hagmans lived in Santa Monica, California.

Kristina came home one day from school and found three teenage boys waiting for her in her room. Her parents were away and the person who was supposed to watch over the two children, wasn't around.

Holding tomatoes he was squishing in his hands, one of the boys told her that her head was going to look like the tomatoes if she refused to do what he told her. Her clothes were off but she doesn't remember how she came to be naked.

'I remember being on the floor looking up at his penis with all the other boys around me. He told me to lick him. I didn't want to, but I did it. I licked him just the way he told me to do. I felt guilty wrong, dirty, bad'.

If she dared to mention the incident to anyone, they promised to come back, tell everyone at school as well as her parents she was a bad girl. And then her parents wouldn't want her anymore.

It instilled fear in her for years and she was afraid to tell her parents. She suffered through headaches and anxiety alone.

'That sense that my home was not a safe place was a feeling I held inside me as long as I lived at home with my parents,' she writes.

But for Hagman, every day was a party day with lots of wine, music, food and a happy family.

Hagman just didn't want to be alone.

He began the party every morning with a beer or vodka and orange juice and then progressed to beer and wine all day.

Their family home was decorated with several flags, which Hagman (pictured with Maj and their children) would often parade around during parties

He started drinking heavily at age fourteen and refused to quit after severe alcohol poisoning in his teens.

Maj drank heavily as well - three to four bottles of wine a day and then a couple of vodkas before bedtime.

But Hagman always outdid her.

The Dallas actor also had a German shepherd puppy, and his quick temper aggressively came out when the dog acted badly. If she barked or chewed anything, he hit her.

His look was frightening when he was hitting the dog. He told his daughter he had to hurt the dog for her own good. 

Hagman never had a close relationship with his mother, Mary Martin, a musical theater star and well known for playing Peter Pan on Broadway. 

But he had a worse relationship with her second husband, Richard Halliday, who Kristina always suspected was gay.

Hagman's daughter, Kristina, revealed father's secrets in her new book, in The Eternal Party: Understanding My Dad, Larry Hagman, the TV Star America Loved to Hate

Halliday moved in and the two never got along so Hagman was shipped off to boarding school for high school. 

When he was caught smoking, he was thrown out and rather than live with Martin and Halliday, he asked to live with his father Ben, well-respected lawyer in Weatherford, Texas.

He hadn't been there since his parents divorced when he was a toddler but he soon learned Ben was the manly man he always wanted to be and they did all 'manly' things together like hunting, smoking and drinking.

With the whole family visiting Hagman's stepmother after his father died, he was so drunk he couldn't see the road ahead of him. 

He opened the driver's side door a crack so that he could follow the yellow line down the center of the road.

'He never stopped drinking with a beer in his hand,' Kristina writes. 'It was dumb luck that he was never in a bad car accident but then in many ways, he was very lucky.' 

Drinking wasn't Hagman's only bad habit at an early age.

Living in New York and getting stage work thanks at first to his mother, he and Maj started taking 'pep pills' and smoking three packs of cigarettes a day.

The pep pills kept them both productive through arduous working schedules.

With television offers in California, the ticket to ride out to the coast was the big offer to act in I Dream of Jeannie, a 1960s fantasy sitcom that starred Barbara Eden.

Eden played a 2000-year old scantily clad genie who falls in love with the US astronaut who finds the bottle with her inside.

Hagman played the astronaut, Tony Nelson.

Always in the shadow of star Eden, Hagman was struggling with an amphetamine addiction as well as a dieting obsession.

He was freaking out and constantly complaining to the producers about lousy scripts.

To calm his nerves from the amphetamine, he smoked three packs of cigarettes a day along with pot until he just went berserk on the set.

On edge with jagged nerves, he thought the air conditioner was too loud. When no one responded to his complaint, he walked over to where the fire retardant and an axe hung on the wall, took down the axe, swung it up and let it land on the electric cord. 

Rather than cutting off the air conditioner, the strike cut off all electricity to the entire soundstage.

He knew he had gone to far and feared he'd never work again.

But his best friend, director Ted Flicker insisted he see a psychiatrist to get things into perspective.

The psychiatrist he visited suggested Hagman take LSD. 

Hagman never had a close relationship with his mother, Mary Martin (left), but she did help him find roles at the start of his career 

Peter Fonda introduced him to singer David Crosby who supplied him with some very high-quality LSD and that began Hagman's love of psychedelics.

'Talk therapy was not going to take him where he wanted to go. And so he turned to LSD to help him allay his anxieties,' Kristina writes, adding that the addictoin went on for years.

Then Jack Nicholson turned Hagman on to pot in 1964 and it became his longest love affair.

While working on the film Ensign Pulver in Acapulco, Mexico, Nicholson decided to turn Hagman on to weed after seeing him drunk too many times at lunch.

After toking up, Hagman told Jack he'd rather have a martini because he still didn't feel anything. He asked Jack just when would the pot kick in, a question he had asked 12 times.

'Dad looked like the happiest man in the world that day in Mexico,' Kristina writes. 

He went out, found Maj and shared it with her.

It was weed that helped Hagman segue in the '60s lifestyle back in California that he loved - the mustache, the flowery peasant shirts, his signature rose-colored glasses.

After his death, Kristina found marijuana brownies in his refrigerator.

Despite Hagman's love for and addiction to pot, he still continued drinking after a liver transplant in 1995.

He never minded being called a pothead but refused to think of himself as a drunk.

'I only drink wine and beer. That's not really drinking,' Kristina remembers her father saying.

The amount of wine consumed in their house was staggering - no less than several bottles of day.

All of their friends drank a lot and Hagman reasoned that drunks did not lead such charmed lives.

Hagman would drink a bottle or two of wine and then go to skid row in downtown Los Angeles and take drunks he found on the streets to sober up at the Midnight Mission.

Growing up, Kristina remembered that the store she shopped most often in with her father was the liquor store.

Hagman, who had a liver transplant in 1995 (pictured showing off his scar), was known to drink heavily throughout his life

The era of the two-martini lunch was still going strong with Hagman's drinking buddies.

His good friend actor Carroll O'Connor, who starred in All in the Family, and lived down the beach from him in Malibu, had a sign in his kitchen, 'Wine kills slowly, and I am not in a hurry'.

If Hagman didn't pass out on city streets, he didn't view his drinking to be a problem.

He just wanted to play, dance, sing, drink and get high.

Every day he carried a little bag filled with his toys – a flute, a Frisbee, a Swiss Army knife that had a corkscrew, a kaleidoscope and his bubble bear that he used to annoy any cigarette smokers by blowing bubbles back at their smoke.

He also carried a string hammock in case he ever got stuck in an airport and need to sleep.

Hagman worried about natural disasters and nuclear holocaust. He had backpacks filled with shoes, eyeglasses, first aid kits, water filters.

He kept stashes of twenty-gallon tubs of dried food and space blankets, cash and a high-frequency radio to be fully prepared for earthquakes or the inevitable tsunami from Japan he believed was going to hit the coast.

For a getaway vehicle, he bought an electric bike that had studded tires and big panniers to hold his emergency gear.

Even at 81 years old, 'he was ready to be a road warrior', Kristina writes.

Hagman spent the last year of his life in Texas and started working on the resurrected Dallas TV series that returned for two years in 2012.

He came back to Los Angeles every few months to visit Maj who had been diagnosed with vascular dementia and was living in a rented home near Hagman's penthouse apartment in Santa Monica.

Diagnosed with leukemia, Hagman died in Dallas in November 2012, when he was 81 years old.

Just before he took his last breath, Kristina heard him asking for forgiveness. She concludes in her book that he was sorry for the angry relationship he sustained with his mother for so many years.

Barry Manilow's ex-wife Susan Deixler on his marriage to Garry Kief

Publish date: 2024-10-05

Barry Manilow's former wife Susan Deixler has spoken out about the superstar crooner's marriage to long-time manager Garry Kief

Barry Manilow's former wife has spoken of her happiness for the superstar crooner now that he has married his long-time gay partner.

Speaking for the first time since news of her former husband's marriage broke Susan Deixler, 70, told Daily Mail Online exclusively: 'I wish him well. I'm happy for him. I'm glad that he's found love and happiness.

'What happened between us, our relationship, is ancient history and I don't want to dig back into ancient history. It's 50 years ago. You have to remember that I'm not a celebrity. I have children, a life…all that was a long time ago.

'I'm a private person but I'm happy for him. I really am.'

Deixler was just 19 when she married Manilow, her high school sweetheart at Eastern District High School, Brooklyn, New York. She was the prettiest girl in the school. His high school yearbook noted that he was the year's best musician.

Manilow recalled Susan in his 1987 autobiography Sweet Life as 'adorable, small with great legs and a voluptuous figure.' He said she had 'jet-black hair, dark brown eyes and a smile that lit up the room.'

The two were married in 1964 before a justice of the peace and then, on her parents' insistence, before a rabbi.

Writing in his autobiography Manilow, who claimed to have fallen 'hard' for Susan admitted that his friends thought the marriage was doomed from the start.

He wrote, 'Even my friends thought I was rushing into it.'

But he walked out on 'the perfect wife' after just one year of marriage telling her that he was going on 'this wondrous musical adventure,' even though he claimed in the book that the one thing he wanted more than success as a singer was for Susan to love him as much as he loved her.

'She reacted badly of course,' he noted. She told him he could only take his clothes, but he insisted on having his piano too and she relented.

Deixler applied for an annulment rather than a divorce after Manilow told her he was going on 'this wondrous musical adventure that I saw within my reach,' he wrote.

But half a century on, the hurt and bitterness Susan felt has faded. 

She qualified as an holistic healer and although she never married she went on to have two children, Pauline, now 41 and Danny, 36.

Whatever went before, now, she insisted, she wishes her former husband 'only the best' in his new marriage to his long-time manager Garry Kief.

Manilow, 71, and Kief, 66, married at their 64-acre compound in Palm Springs, California in April last year, but news of the nuptials only broke this week as they close in on their first anniversary.

They did not file papers and so the marriage is not considered legally binding.

The ceremony, presided over by Manilow's long-time assistant Mark Hulett with neighbor Suzanne Somers acting as best man, was kept so secret that many of Manilow's best friends are only learning about it now.

Even some of Manilow's closest friends were completely unaware that the superstar had decided to go ahead and marry the man who has been his partner and manager for more than three decades.

'I find it hard to believe it happened a year ago and no-one has told me,' long-time writing partner Marty Panzer told Daily Mail Online in an exclusive interview. 'I'm not saying it didn't happen but I can't believe I haven't heard of it before now.'

'It's bizarre that I haven't heard anything, but everyone goes through periods of time when they are closer to people or further apart and I haven't spoken to Barry in 7 or 8 months, added Panzer, whose credits for Manilow include New York Rhythm and I Am Your Child.

Panzer, who described the Copacabana crooner as his 'best friend' said it is not unusual for him to go several months without having contact with the superstar singer when they are not working together. 

'Sometimes we speak 30 or 40 times a day and sometimes we don't speak for months.

'Barry has been busy. He's been on tour and before that he was in the studio making his new album — which wasn't original songs, so I wasn't involved.'

Manilow recalled Susan in his 1987 autobiography Sweet Life as 'adorable, small with great legs and a voluptuous figure.' He said she had 'jet-black hair, dark brown eyes and a smile that lit up the room'

Panzer said he is happy for the couple, but not all friends share that view. One told Daily Mail Online that Kief is something of a 'Svengali' to Manilow and has pushed people out of the multi-millionaire singer's life and replaced them with his own relatives over the years.

That friend said it is Kief who didn't want to be 'Mrs Manilow'. He resisted marriage for so long and persuaded the superstar that it would hurt his standing among his fans — who are overwhelmingly middle aged and female.

'Also Garry felt that he would lose respect if they got married. He didn't want to be known as Mrs. Manilow.'

But last year, with Manilow's health failing, Kief agreed to the commitment ceremony, said the friend. 

Manilow is afflicted with atrial fibrillation, a cardiac condition which leads to irregular heartbeat.

 Manilow, 71, and Kief, 66 (pictured in November 2014) married at their 64-acre compound in Palm Springs

'You think you're gonna die. You're not gonna die, but you think you are,' he told Seven Magazine in 2012. 'Heart pounding like crazy. It's like you have a flounder in your jacket. You think, 'Well, any second now it's just gonna explode.'

'I got used to it, but they'd rush me to the hospital every time,' Manilow added. 'Give you the paddles – 'clear!' 'Cause they have to stop it. If it goes on for a couple of days they have to stop it.'

He also suffers from serious hip problems that he blames on '30 years of jigging around on stage to Copacabana.' A seven-hour operation in 2011 left him in a wheelchair for two months.

Like Manilow, Kief was also married to a woman. He has a 38-year-old daughter, Kristen, who works in the Manilow organization.

The singer has long dodged questions about his sexuality. He is known for being so obsessively private that he won't even reveal his dogs' names. Even when Patricia Butler's 2002 biography outed him as gay he did not address the issue.

The book's publisher Chris Charlesworth told Daily Mail Online that lawyers at the time advised him to take out all reference to Manilow's sexuality.

In his blog, Charlesworth wrote: 'I protested. Not only was it fairly well known within the music industry that Manilow was gay but my author had done her research well and come up with quotes from friends and professional colleagues that confirmed Manilow was a gay man.'

Eventually, Charlesworth said, they decided to 'publish and be damned.' 'As I thought, Manilow didn't sue us,' he said.

Charlesworth said the lawyer's advice was based on the fact that 'Manilow's fan base consisted primarily of single ladies of a certain age who believed he was 'available,' that this was part of his attraction as a singer and that the book could therefore be accused of implying that Manilow as being 'deceitful,' and that this — not the actual allegation that he was gay — was libelous.'

Butler, the biography's Chicago-based author, told Daily Mail Online that when she started writing the book she had not realized that Manilow was gay, but her research confirmed it for her.

Butler said she suspects that the reason to marry now was because society's attitudes toward homosexuality has relaxed so much over the past few years.

'When the book came out I got quite a violent backlash from a good portion of his fans who called me a liar and were in complete denial,' Butler told Daily Mail Online. 'They took it so personally.

'The one thing I hope is that the reason it has now come out is because Barry and Garry decided to leak it themselves and not that their secret has been disseminated for them,' added Butler.

'But either way, it's kind of nice that they are dealing with it a little more openly.'

Manilow's camp is still refusing to comment on the ceremony, which was first reported by the National Enquirer. 

The only oblique reference from the couple was a Thursday Twitter comment from Kief, which merely said: 'LOL…so how was YOUR day?' Kief has since taken his Twitter feed private. 

Panzer said he is always amazed how Manilow, 71, and Kief, 66, have stayed together so long in a business as well as a personal relationship. 'They have been together for 35 years. How many people stay together for 35 weeks these days?

'I don't know anyone who has been in a professional relationship with their partner for so long.'

'It's a credit to them. I am happy for Barry that he has been so well taken care of by Garry for all these years. His career has lasted 40 years and Garry has been with him for more than 30 of them.'

Manilow's refusal to confirm his homosexuality has brought criticism from the LGBT community. As far back as 2002, the website PrideSource called him 'gay as a picnic basket and queer as a three-dollar bill.'

The site took him to task for talking about a duet he performed on stage with Broadway star Brian D'Arcy James: 'Of course, we're not going to sing it to each other — that would be creepy,' Manilow told the audience at New York's Madison Square Garden.

PrideSource hit back. 'Creepy for two men to sing to each other? No, Mr. Copacabana, it's creepy for you to say that it would be.'

Ed Shapiro, a schoolfriend of Manilow's, told Daily Mail Online that it was a 'difficult thing to be gay' at their high school in 1961, and that one of their classmates was bullied for it. 

'There was one guy who was locked up in lockers and picked on because he was outwardly gay,' he said. They locked him overnight in the gym locker room. He was an exhibitionist kind of guy.

'It was a difficult thing to be gay at high school in 1961, not a good thing to broadcast.

'There was a lot of super macho guys and they took a very dim view of that, they would call him bad names.'

Recalling Barry as a schoolboy, Ed said: 'Even at that stage he might have been quite involved in music, or piano, I was interested in music as well but not to that extend.

'I don't remember him socializing much at school, I just remember he was a thin, good looking kid.He was always a thin guy.

'I vaguely recall that he was teased for being thin, I don't know it was a major thing, just locker room. I don't remember him swaggering around the school, or anything like that. He kept a low profile as far as I can remember.

'I don't remember him, or even in his career, acting effeminate or anything like that.'

Ed didn't recall Susan, who was a year younger than Barry, but said if they were romantically involved at school he didn't remember.

He said: 'Back in the day when someone was romantically involved with another student, they wouldn't exactly walk down the hall hand in hand, they were discouraged. We are talking a long time ago, the New York City Board of education was conservative.'

Asked if he thought the bullying of the gay pupil affected Barry, Ed said: 'I don't know at what point people discover this. I know people have got married knowing they were gay but then wanted to fit in with the rest of the gang and got married and that didn't work out, I don't know if that's the case with Barry, I have no idea. I think there was a general stigma. Everything had a stigma, racial stuff, all kinds of crazy things, so that was a big one (being gay).

'The school was a little bit ghettoized, there were cliques, people stuck together, perhaps there wasn't much interaction between the abusive people and nice people. The ones that were decidedly different, they would be loners or just clique off and hang together.

'Unfortunately it still exists today, people still do that. You would think it would be passé by now, but people still carry it on in one way or another.'

Ed, now a photographer in Canada, also congratulated his former school pal, adding: 'Why should he have to hide or anything like that. I would say congratulations to Barry. Basically he's been a good citizen, he's well known and successful, why should he not have a normal life finally.' 

And despite Kief's fears that Manilow's fans would desert him if they went public with their relationship, the online reaction has been overwhelmingly positive as well. 

'I don't care what his sexuality is,' Wendy Ritson wrote on Manilow's Facebook page. 'Have followed & loved him & his music since the 70's & nothing is going to change that now. Congratulations to you both.'

And Kathryn Mills added: 'Who cares if BM is gay, always known, never cared, talent is talent sexuality doesn't matter.'

Others begged him to come clean. 'Could you please somehow text everybody…stating if you're married or not,' wrote Myrna Robinson. 'I love to hear it come out of your mouth that you got married.'
